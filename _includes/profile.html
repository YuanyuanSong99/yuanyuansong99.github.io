I'm a postdoc at MIT, working with Gaël Forget in Mick Follow's group.

I'm interested in climate change. My research involves dynamics in atmosphere and ocean, especially air-sea interaction under global warming.

Research Interests
Atmosphere blockings

Atmospheric blockings are linked to Arctic warming and sea ice melting, as well as heatwaves and extreme cold events in mid-latitudes.

Southern Ocean warming

The Southern Ocean has been rapidly changing over the past decades with widespread consequences for the global climate.

Mode waters

Mode waters are subsurface ocean water masses with nearly uniform temperature with a thickness of a few hundred meters.

Ocean: Southern Ocean temperature change

The Southern Ocean (SO) has witnessed substantial warming trends over the past half-century, which accounts for ~60% of the global heat storage in the upper 2000 m. The SO warming has significant ramifications on sea ice and ice shelves, the formation of abyssal waters, marine ecosystems and biodiversity. Here, we reveal a notable inter-basin contrast in the SO warming rates. Over the past six decades (1960-2020), the 0-700 m warming in the Atlantic-Indian sector has been ~40.0% faster than in the Pacific sector, nearly doubling the rate at the warming maximum of ~44°S. Analysis of model simulations indicates that this feature arises from heat redistribution driven by changes in wind-driven ocean circulation, rather than differences in surface heating (Song et al. 2025(under review)). In addition to long-term warming trend, we also find an inter-basin temperature oscillation between the Atlantic-Indian and Pacific sector on the decadal time scale. The Interdecadal Pacific Oscillation (IPO) has been found to act as a key role in driving this SO temperature decadal variability based on the Pacific pacemaker and preindustrial simulations of CESM1 (Song et al. 2024). These two findings on inter-basin contrast complement prior knowledge of the meridional temperature change pattern in the SO, offering a new perspective on its zonal distribution and deepening our understanding of three-dimensional temperature change structure.
